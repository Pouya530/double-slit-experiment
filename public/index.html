About

An interactive 3D web application that visualises the quantum double-slit experiment.

Users fire photons or electrons at a barrier with two slits and watch an interference pattern build on a detection screen.

The double-slit experiment sends quantum particles through two narrow openings; when they are not measured at the slits, they behave like waves and produce bright and dark bands on a screen from interference. When which-path information is introduced, the pattern shifts toward two lumped bands—classic evidence of wave–particle duality.

This app’s objective is to make those ideas tangible: explore the bench in three dimensions, scrub time to see hits accumulate, toggle observer-style measurements, and adjust slit geometry and wavelength so the physics is clear for students and teachers.

FAQ

Answers for students, teachers, and anyone new to the simulation—drawn from how the experiment, demo, and classroom constraints are defined for this project.

What is the double-slit experiment?

Quantum particles (e.g. photons or electrons) are directed at a barrier with two narrow openings and detected on a screen behind it. When paths are not resolved at the slits, a statistical pattern of hits can show interference—bright and dark regions—consistent with wave behaviour. The app places you at a 3D bench with source, slits, and screen so that layout is obvious.

What is wave–particle duality?

The same physical description can exhibit particle-like detection (discrete hits) and wave-like statistics (interference), depending on the experimental arrangement—including whether which-path information is available. The double-slit setup is the standard introduction to that idea at A-level.

What does the observer toggle do in the demo?

With observation emphasised, the pattern moves toward two lumped bands behind the slits—an intuitive stand-in for classical which-path behaviour. With observation off, the distribution returns toward an interference-like pattern. It is a teaching visualisation, not a full quantum measurement model.

How does the timeline scrubber work?

Impacts are pre-computed into a buffer. The timeline changes current time: only particles whose emission time is on or before that instant are shown. Scrubbing left “rewinds” the pattern; scrubbing right builds it up again—without recomputing everything until you change physical parameters.

Which parameters can I usually change?

Typical controls include slit width, slit separation, wavelength (often tied to colour), emission rate, and distance to the screen. Together they link geometry and λ to fringe spacing—exactly what many exam specs ask students to reason about.

Is the physics in the demo “exact”?

The teaching model uses the standard double-slit intensity form: an interference term multiplied by a single-slit diffraction (sinc²) envelope. With observation on, a classical mixture (e.g. Gaussians behind each slit) replaces fringe structure. That matches how interference and diffraction are introduced in school—not a complete quantum measurement or decoherence simulation.

Who is this for?

A-level physics (16–18) is the core audience—the practical appears across AQA, OCR, Edexcel, and WJEC. It also supports GCSE scaffolding, museums (short interaction loops), and non-specialist teachers—about a quarter of English state secondaries lack a specialist physics teacher, so clear visuals matter.

Will it run on classroom Chromebooks?

UK schools often use modest Chromebooks (e.g. Intel Celeron class, 4 GB RAM, integrated graphics). The product roadmap includes adaptive quality: fewer particles, simpler materials, and no heavy post-processing on low tiers so the demo stays usable at ~30 fps on that class of hardware.

How is this different from PhET or other sims?

PhET has huge reach and excellent 2D wave labs. This project adds a 3D bench, timeline scrubber, observer-style toggle, and orbital camera so the experiment reads as a spatial apparatus—not only a flat interference plot.

WebGPU vs WebGL—what should I expect?

Where supported, WebGPU enables higher particle counts and compute-oriented paths; otherwise the app falls back to WebGL 2 with lower caps. You do not choose manually—the runtime picks a safe path for the device.

Is it free? What about privacy and analytics?

The experience is free in the browser. Analytics are intended to be cookieless and aggregated, aligned with ICO expectations for young users—no engagement gimmicks, streaks, or personal profiling in the product principles for this build.

What accessibility features are targeted?

The specification aims for WCAG 2.2 AA: keyboard routes for 3D controls, visible focus, live announcements for important simulation changes, and non-canvas alternatives (e.g. tabular summaries) where helpful. Marketing pages honour prefers-reduced-motion where motion is decorative.

Can teachers share one exact setup?

Yes—state can be encoded in the URL so you can pass a link with specific parameters (slits, wavelength, etc.) to a class, aligned with “shareable experiment state” in the product plan.

When "observing," the interference pattern collapses to two classical bands. Toggle off and the wave pattern returns. The "wow" moment — students see wave function collapse in real time.
